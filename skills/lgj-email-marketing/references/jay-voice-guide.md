# Jay Feldman (Lead Gen Jay) — Voice Reference Guide

Use this guide to write emails that sound authentically like Jay. Every email should pass one test: would Jay actually say this to a friend who runs an agency?

---

## Core Voice Principles

Jay's voice is built on four pillars:

1. **Authority without arrogance** — He references real results (eight-figure agencies, million-dollar monthly revenue, massive communities) early, then moves straight into value. He never brags without purpose.
2. **Earned trust** — Value always precedes the ask. Never push before you've given something useful.
3. **Street-level directness** — Talks like a smart friend, not a consultant. Short sentences. No fluff.
4. **Proof over promises** — Specific numbers beat vague claims every time. "$40k/month" beats "significant revenue."

---

## Opening Hooks

Always open by challenging something the reader believes or setting up a problem they recognize.

**Patterns that work:**
- Challenge a common belief: "I hear this every single year — [wrong belief]. And it's costing people a lot of money."
- Pose a hypothetical: "Imagine you land a $10k/month client tomorrow. What's your first move?"
- Call out the contradiction: "Everyone says [X]. Almost nobody actually does it."
- Reference the moment: "It's [month]. The agencies that haven't figured out [X] yet are about to feel it."

**Then:** Brief authority anchor (one sentence, one proof point), then straight into the content.

---

## Argument Structure

Use the problem-solution framework for every teaching email.

```
Hook (challenge belief or name the pain)
  ↓
"See, here's the problem..." — name what most people get wrong
  ↓
"Here's what actually works..." — the real answer
  ↓
"Now here's what you do..." — concrete steps
  ↓
CTA — action, not pressure
```

**Transitional anchors:**
- "Now" — moves between steps, keeps flow
- "See, here's the thing..." — pivoting to the real insight
- "The truth is..." — signaling honest advice after framing the BS version
- "So what do I mean by that?" — rhetorical, used when introducing a concept

**Rhetorical questions:** Use sparingly, max one or two per email. They should feel natural, not like a copywriting trick.

---

## Signature Phrases and When to Use Them

| Phrase | Use When |
|--------|----------|
| "The Sauce" | Referring to high-value insider strategies: "This is the sauce most agencies never find." |
| "Superpowers" / "Cheat Code" | Describing leverage — a tool, skill, or system that multiplies output: "AI outreach is a legitimate cheat code right now." |
| "Insane" | Genuine emphasis on impressive results: "The retention numbers we saw were insane." |
| "BS" | Calling out bad advice or fake gurus, no softening: "Most of what you read about cold email is BS." |
| "Getting after it" | Execution energy, usually in a CTA or sign-off context: "Hope you're getting after it this week." |
| "The truth is..." | Pivoting to honest advice, especially when contradicting popular opinion. |
| "Unless you hate money" | Sign-off hook for subscribe/opt-in CTAs. Use sparingly — it loses impact if overused. |

**Never use:** leverage, synergize, utilize, optimize, cutting-edge, robust, seamlessly, unlock your potential.

---

## Sentence Structure Rules

- Default paragraph length: 1-3 sentences.
- Default sentence length: 10-20 words.
- Use fragments when they land a point: "Simple. But most people skip it."
- Always first person "I," always direct address "you."
- Active voice only. "I built" not "was built." "You'll close" not "deals will be closed."
- Specific over vague: "$8,400/month" not "good revenue." "17 agencies" not "many agencies."

## Tone by Context

### Teaching (Nurture / Value Emails)

Patient but direct. Step-by-step. The reader should feel like Jay is pulling back the curtain.

**Sample paragraph:**

> Here's what actually works for cold email right now. You're not trying to sell on the first touch — you're just trying to start a conversation. One line about their business. One specific problem you've seen in their industry. One ask: "Worth a 15-minute chat?" That's it. I've seen that three-sentence sequence book more meetings than any fancy copy I've ever written.

---

### Selling (Promo / Offer Emails)

Confident, proof-heavy. State the offer clearly. Don't apologize for selling. The value was established first.

**Sample paragraph:**

> I opened enrollment for [Program Name] to 20 people this week. Last cohort, the average student added $12k/month to their retainer revenue in 90 days. That's not a promise — that's what the data showed. If you've been thinking about it, now's the time. Spots close Friday and I'm not doing a countdown timer about it. Either it's a fit or it isn't.

---

### Storytelling (Personal / Authority Emails)

Vivid and specific. Use real details — ages, dollar amounts, places, names. The story earns the lesson.

**Sample paragraph:**

> When I was 17, I made a decision that I'd never let money control my life again. Not because I had some grand vision — because I watched my dad lose his job and couldn't do anything about it. That feeling stuck with me longer than anything else. It's why I built Lead Gen Jay the way I did: so nobody who works with me ever has to feel that helpless again.

---

### Calling Out BS (Pattern Interrupt / Re-engagement Emails)

No hedging. No softening. Direct and a little provocative. This earns credibility because Jay says what others won't.

**Sample paragraph:**

> Let me be real. Most cold email courses will teach you to send 1,000 emails a day and call it a system. That's not a system — that's spam with a spreadsheet. The agencies killing it right now are sending 50 emails a day, hyper-personalized, to a list they actually researched. Boring? Yes. Effective? Insane results. The gurus won't tell you this because it doesn't scale into a $997 course.

## Sign-Offs by Email Type

| Email Type | Sign-Off |
|------------|----------|
| Nurture / value email | `— Jay` |
| Sales / promo email | `Jay Feldman, Lead Gen Jay` |
| Broadcast / weekly blast | `Talk soon,`<br>`Jay` |
| Final sequence email | `Jay Feldman`<br>`Lead Gen Jay`<br>`P.S. [hook that restates the offer or opens a loop]` |

**P.S. usage:** Always use a P.S. in final sequence emails. It should restate the most compelling point or open a loop the reader has to click to close.

---

## What Jay Never Does

- Corporate language (leverage, synergize, utilize, optimize)
- Passive voice
- Hedging words: maybe, perhaps, I think, it could be, potentially
- Over-promising without proof to back it
- Hard selling before delivering value
- Heavy emoji use (one per email max, and only if it genuinely fits)
- Long paragraphs (5+ sentences)
- Vague claims ("many clients," "significant results," "a lot of money")

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before sending any email written in Jay's voice, verify:

- [ ] Opens with a hook that challenges or names a problem
- [ ] No sentence longer than 25 words
- [ ] No paragraph longer than 3 sentences
- [ ] At least one specific number (dollar amount, timeframe, or metric)
- [ ] Zero corporate vocabulary
- [ ] No hedging language
- [ ] CTA is clear, single, and not pushy
- [ ] Sign-off matches email type
- [ ] Would pass the "friend" test — sounds like a smart friend, not a marketer
