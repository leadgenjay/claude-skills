# Open-Rate Playbook

> Distilled from Jay Feldman's client-clinic call fixing three teams' bad cold emails
> (LTL freight, conversion tracking, insurance). Read this before writing any copy.

## The one law everything serves

**A prospect sees only two things before deciding to open: the subject line and the first line
(preview text). They are the ONLY levers for opens.** If either telegraphs "this is a cold email,"
the prospect deletes it and a reply is impossible.

> "We have to make them curious enough to open it, and the only chance we have to do that is this
> first sentence and the subject line. If they're not opening it, they've got no chance of
> responding to it." — Jay

Corollary: **never blindly ship AI-generated copy.** Read the subject + first line as the
prospect. If you think "that sounds like a cold email, I wouldn't open that," don't send it.

---

## Rule 1 — Don't telegraph that it's a cold email

This is the single biggest failure mode. The moment the subject or preview reads as outreach, it's
deleted.

- **Kill the research flex.** Openers built to "prove you researched them" are the worst offenders:
  - ❌ "Curious about [Company]'s recent LTL experience"
  - ❌ "When [Company] scales ad spend, does the cost per booked call hold steady or quietly climb?"
  - Both scream cold email and never get opened.
- **No bait-for-bait's-sake.** A bare first name or a standalone "quick question" baits opens but
  is lazy. The bait must relate to what's actually inside the email so the open is honest and the
  body pays off. "Quick question" is only acceptable when it is **fused to a specific hook in the
  same line** (e.g. "quick question, are you still using FedEx for your LTL?") — never on its own
  and never as the subject line.

## Rule 2 — Sound like a vendor, a client, or a friend (not a salesperson)

Write the subject so it could plausibly be from someone the prospect already deals with.

- Diagnostic / found-a-problem (feels like a vendor or colleague):
  "Found a pixel issue" · "Tracking glitch found" · "Who set up your tracking?"
- Personal / handshake (feels like a friend):
  "NAME, it's Austin" · "Do you have an insurance guy?"

## Rule 3 — The `Re:` trick

Prepend `Re:` so the subject reads like a reply in an existing thread. Especially effective in
industries not used to cold email. Use it honestly — the follow-ups genuinely thread.

- "Re: Latest LTL quote" · "Your LTL quote" · "Using FedEx?"

## Rule 4 — First lines provoke curiosity or mild confusion

The first line doubles as preview text. Its only job is to earn the open by creating a small
question the prospect must resolve by reading.

- Curiosity / confusion: "Did you ask for this LTL quote from FedEx?" (→ "wait, what?")
- Show-me question: "Does this look like your latest LTL shipping quote?" (they want to check)
- Fix-framing statement: "We fixed your LTL shipping quote."
- Diagnostic claim: "I don't think your server-side tracking is set up correctly." /
  "Did you know your website isn't firing any offline conversions?"
- Warm/familiar (no-hook): "What's up NAME! Long time no see." / "Hope all's well, my friend."

Each is a smooth transition into the problem, but earns the open first.

**Pain belongs in sentence 2, not the first line.** When you have rich pain points (e.g. from a
strategy doc), do NOT open by stating the pain ("Most agencies... is killing your engagement") —
that's a pitch and it telegraphs. Turn the pain into a curiosity hook or diagnostic question, earn
the open, then explain the pain in the body. Pain = raw material for the hook, never the hook itself.

## Rule 5 — CTA gives the fix, not "let me share what we do"

Founders who can't do the thing themselves want the fix, or to be handed the fix — not a
description of your process.

- ❌ "Mind if I send a quick rundown on how our quotes work?"
- ❌ "Want me to share what we're doing for tracking?"
- ✅ "Can I share a quick fix for your offline tracking?"
- ✅ "Can I send you a link to compare LTL rates across all carriers? (no login needed)"
- ✅ Easy-out variants: "Are you still using FedEx for LTL?" · "Who's your go-to for LTL shipping?"

Keep it answerable with a simple "yes." Give them one thing to reply to, or there's nothing to
respond to and reply rate is zero.

## Rule 6 — A PS line to de-risk (optional but powerful)

Remove the reason to say no, or add reassurance:

- "PS: you can check our site as many times as you want for the lowest quotes, no login needed."
- "PS: if you already have someone you love and trust, then no worries."

## Rule 7 — Never a blob of text

Break every email into short lines with white space between thoughts. A wall of text reads as
automated and doesn't get read. (Jay flagged "one block of text" twice on the call.)

## Rule 8 — The no-hook personality fallback

When there's **no lead magnet, no unique mechanism, and no social proof**, do not force a
proof-driven email. Go short, personal, high-volume, handshake-style — like a note from a friend.
Lead entirely on personality and friendliness. **Do not invent social proof.**

## Worked examples (before → after)

### Example A — LTL freight (Andre)

Problem: preview text "Curious about [Company]'s recent LTL experience" telegraphed a cold email.

- **Subjects:** `Your LTL quote` // `Re: Latest LTL quote` // `Using FedEx?`
- **First lines:** "Did you ask for this LTL quote from FedEx?" // "Does this look like your latest
  LTL shipping quote?" // "We fixed your LTL shipping quote."
- **CTAs:** "Would love to know, what site are you currently using to compare LTL rates across
  multiple carriers?" // "Are you still using FedEx for LTL shipping?" // "Who is your go-to for
  LTL shipping?" // "Can I send you a link to compare LTL rates across all carriers? (no login
  needed)"
- **PS:** "You can check our site as many times as you want for the lowest quotes and don't need a
  login or anything."

### Example B — Conversion tracking (Dor) — diagnostic-fix

Problem: copy offered to "share what we do for tracking" instead of fixing it; CTA didn't help a
founder who doesn't know how to set up tracking. ICP was too broad (industry-agnostic, CMO).

- **ICP tightening:** 20-30 employees MAX; CEO / OWNER / FOUNDER only.
- **Subjects:** `Tracking glitch found` // `Found a pixel issue` // `Who set up your tracking?`
- **First lines:** "Did you know your website is not firing any offline conversions?" //
  "I don't think your CAPI / server-side tracking is set up correctly."
- **CTAs:** "Can I share a quick fix for your offline tracking?" // "Do you have someone that knows
  how to configure your server-side tracking? If not I'd love to do that for you, should only take
  an hour or so."

### Example C — Insurance (Austin) — no-hook personality fallback

Problem: nothing unique, no lead magnet, no social proof. Old subject "If I could get your company
a better rate on insurance, would you be open to seeing a couple options?" was deleted on sight.
Fix: heavy personality, friendliness, handshake tone.

- **Subjects:** `NAME, it's Austin` // `Do you have an insurance guy?` // `Recommendation for an
  insurance guy?`
- **First line:** "What's up NAME! Long time no see." // "Hope all is going well my friend."
- **Body:** "We haven't met, but my name is Austin and I'd love to be your 'guy' for all things
  insurance. I'm reliable and you seem like a cool person to work with so figured I'd reach out."
- **CTA:** "Mind if I send my cell number in case you need me in the future?"
- **PS:** "If you already have someone you love and trust, then no worries."

## Pre-send gate (the "would I open this?" test)

Before shipping, read ONLY the subject + first line of each email, as the prospect:

1. Would you open it, or does it smell like a cold email? If it smells, rewrite.
2. Is the bait tied to the real content, or bait-for-bait's-sake?
3. Does the CTA give the fix and ask for a simple yes?
4. Is the body short lines with white space, not a blob?
5. If there's no real proof, did you switch to the personality fallback instead of faking it?
